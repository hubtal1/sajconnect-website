The question comes up in almost every first conversation about factory connectivity: "Do we really need private 5G — or is modern Wi-Fi enough?" The honest answer: it depends on your processes, not on the technology. Both worlds have moved on, and anyone giving you a blanket recommendation today either has something to sell or hasn't looked closely.

Here is the decision framework we use ourselves.

## Where Wi-Fi 6E wins

Wi-Fi remains the most economical solution for many applications — and with 6E (the additional 6 GHz band) it has caught up exactly where legacy WLAN used to fail on the shop floor: more spectrum, less overlap, less interference.

**Wi-Fi is usually the right choice when:**

- your devices speak Wi-Fi anyway (laptops, tablets, scanners, printers) — the ecosystem is unbeatably broad and affordable
- your applications are **stationary or slow-moving**: workstations, terminals, machine-mounted sensors
- the area is manageable and can be covered with a properly planned access-point grid
- your IT team already runs WLAN — no new operating model, no SIM management

In short: for the "office inside the plant" and stationary data connectivity, Wi-Fi 6E is hard to beat.

## Where private 5G wins

A campus network plays its strengths where WLAN hits physical and conceptual limits:

- **Mobility with hard requirements.** Automated guided vehicles (AGVs), forklift terminals, mobile robotics: a 5G connection survives cell changes without dropping. Wi-Fi roaming — even with 802.11r — is a common source of trouble in practice, precisely when the device is moving, i.e. at the most critical moment.
- **Licensed spectrum.** In a dedicated band, you are the only one transmitting. No neighbour, no guest device, no microwave oven. For processes that must behave deterministically, interference sovereignty is the real reason to buy.
- **Large and difficult areas.** Outdoor yards, high-bay warehouses, metal-heavy environments: 5G covers with far fewer radio cells than a Wi-Fi grid — and outdoor use of the 6 GHz band is regulatorily constrained anyway.
- **Prioritisation and quality of service.** If the emergency-stop command and a software update share the same network, you want to guarantee who gets priority. QoS is an architectural principle in 5G, not an accessory.
- **SIM-based identity.** A device without your SIM does not get on the network — a different security model from any password- or certificate-based WLAN.

## The truth about cost — on both sides

Money is rarely compared fairly. The items that tend to be missing from proposals:

**For private 5G:** end devices and routers with 5G modules cost noticeably more than their Wi-Fi counterparts. SIM and profile management is a new operational process. And a campus network needs to be operated — "who does day-2?" belongs in every calculation. The spectrum licence itself is usually the smallest item: a modest administrative fee depending on area and bandwidth.

**For Wi-Fi:** in demanding halls the access-point grid gets dense — cabling, switches and installation add up. And the cost of recurring radio interference appears in no proposal; it shows up in operations as downtime, troubleshooting and frustration.

## The checklist for your decision

1. Do your critical devices move — and does a process break if the connection stalls for 2 seconds?
2. Do you share the radio environment with neighbours, guests or your own legacy systems?
3. Do outdoor areas, yards or high-bay warehouses need coverage?
4. Are there applications that need guaranteed latency or prioritisation?
5. Are your end devices even available with 5G modules — or would everything be a retrofit?
6. Who operates the network in three years — your team, a partner, the vendor?

Three or more "yes" answers on questions 1–4: run the numbers on private 5G seriously. Mostly "no" plus question 5 against you: a well-planned Wi-Fi 6E will make you happier at lower cost.

## The realistic answer is often: both

Most factories we see end up hybrid: Wi-Fi for the office, commodity devices and stationary connectivity — private 5G for the mobile and critical processes. What matters is not the religious question of "5G or WLAN" but a clean mapping: which process needs which properties, and what does an outage actually cost?
